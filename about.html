History

The origin of Fushimi Inari Taisha is described in Yamashirokoku Fudoki, an ancient report on provincial culture, geography and oral tradition that was presented to the emperor. Irogu no Hatanokimi, an ancestor of Hatanonakatsue no Imiki, is said to have shot a rice cake, which turned into a swan and flew away. Eventually the swan landed on a peak of a mountain, where an auspicious omen occurred and rice grew. Inari is named for this miracle (“ina” is Japanese for “rice”). It has also been described in other ancient texts, which state that priests such as Hatauji have held spring and autumn festivals at the shrine ever since the deity Inari Okami was enshrined on a plateau in the Inari Mitsugamine area during the Wado era (708-715).

The shrine became the object of imperial patronage during the early Heian period. In 965, Emperor Murakami decreed that messengers carry written accounts of important events to the guardian kami of Japan. These heihaku were initially presented to 16 shrines, including the Inari Shrine.

From 1871 through 1946, Fushimi Inari-taisha was officially designated one of the Kanpei-taisha (官幣大社), meaning that it stood in the first rank of government supported shrines.

Shrines

The main shrine building (also known as Honden) contains five shrines in worship of five Kami that embody the great virtues of Inari Okami. The center shrine, which is referred to as the Lower Shrine, embodies Ukanomitama no Okami; to the north (left) of the Lower shrine is the Middle Shrine, which embodies Satahiko no Okami; To the south (right) of the of the Lower shrine is the Upper Shrine for Omiyanome no Okami; and at the northern end and southern end are the auxiliary shrines of Tanaka no Okami and Shino Okami, respectively.
